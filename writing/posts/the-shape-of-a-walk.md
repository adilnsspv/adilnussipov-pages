title: The Shape of a Walk
description: On walking as a method of thinking, and what the city teaches if you let it.

I do my best thinking on foot. Not while listening to anything—no podcasts, no music, nothing with words in it. Just walking, with the ambient noise of wherever I am as a kind of white noise that keeps one part of my brain occupied so the other part can work.

I don't fully understand the mechanism, but I've come to depend on it. Something about the rhythmic motion, the slight engagement required to navigate terrain, the way the visual field changes continuously—it seems to loosen things. Problems that felt solid and immovable when I was sitting at a desk become pliable when I'm walking. I find angles I didn't know were there.

## The City as Text

A city is densely layered with information. Walk down any street and you're reading—not consciously, but your nervous system is processing a continuous stream of signals. The texture of a sidewalk. The way a building's façade has been maintained or let go. The presence or absence of people, and what that suggests about the time, the neighborhood, the weather. The small evidence of lives being lived: a bicycle locked to a railing, a window box, a hand-lettered sign.

Most of this processing happens below conscious attention. But it feeds upward. I've often found that a long walk changes my mood in ways I can't trace back to any specific thing I saw. The city was talking and I was half-listening.

The walks I remember best are the ones where I got slightly lost. Not dangerously lost—just took a wrong turn, ended up on a street I didn't recognize, had to recalibrate. There's a particular quality of attention that comes with mild disorientation. You notice more. You stop navigating on autopilot and start actually looking at where you are.

## Thinking Through the Body

Descartes drew a hard line between the thinking mind and the extended body. We've largely kept this as a cultural inheritance: thinking is what happens in the head; the body is just transport. But there's a growing body of evidence—and a much longer tradition of philosophical argument—that cognition is thoroughly embodied. We don't just think with our brains; we think with our whole sensorimotor system.

Walking might be the most primitive technology for thinking. Before writing, before any external memory system, there were feet and a landscape to move through. The act of traversing physical space may have co-evolved with the act of mentally traversing conceptual space. When I talk about an argument *holding together*, or an idea *coming apart*, or a conclusion that *doesn't follow*—I'm using spatial metaphors for something that isn't spatial at all.

Maybe the body keeps score of the metaphors. Maybe walking is where they live.

## The Non-Destination

There's a distinction between a walk and a commute. A commute is about getting somewhere; walking is about the movement itself. The best walks have destinations loose enough that you can vary the route, dawdle, stop. They leave room for the walk to take you somewhere you didn't plan to go.

I've started thinking about this as a design problem. Most of my time is scheduled, purposeful, optimized in ways that leave no room for the adjacent possible. A walk creates that room. It's unstructured time that nevertheless produces things—not reliably, not on command, but consistently enough that I've come to see it as real work.

The notebooks I've filled walking are full of half-formed ideas, questions without answers, sentences that point toward something I can't yet articulate. These are not waste. They're the raw material of everything I eventually write. The walk is where it starts.

Go out without a destination. Get a little lost. Come back different.
