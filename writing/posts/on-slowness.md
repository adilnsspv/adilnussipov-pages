---
title: On Slowness
description: Why working at a fraction of possible speed is sometimes the only way forward.
date: 2025-04-12
---

There is a kind of work that cannot be rushed. Not because of external constraints—deadlines, resources, complexity—but because the thinking itself requires duration. The mind needs time to move around an idea, to find its edges, to discover which parts are real and which are scaffolding you put up to feel like you were making progress.

I've been thinking about this since a conversation last winter with a friend who builds furniture by hand. He told me that the slowest part of making a chair isn't the joinery or the finishing—it's the hour he sits with the wood before he picks up any tool. He's looking for something, though he can't always say what. A defect, maybe. Or a grain that wants to go a particular direction. He's learning the material before he asks anything of it.

Most software I use is designed to eliminate that hour.

## The Illusion of Optimization

We've built an entire culture around the premise that speed is a proxy for quality. Fast iteration, rapid prototyping, move fast and break things. The metaphors are all kinetic. And they're not wrong—there are domains where speed genuinely beats deliberation, where the feedback loop is short enough that you can learn through doing rather than thinking.

But there's a category of work where speed is actively destructive. Writing is one of them. So is anything that requires you to hold a complex structure in your head long enough to notice when something is off.

When I was writing code professionally, I noticed that my best work came in long, uninterrupted sessions that felt almost meditative. Not because I was working slowly in the sense of being leisurely, but because I'd stopped switching contexts. The work had my full attention, and attention takes time to settle. It's like letting sediment fall to the bottom of a glass of water: you can't rush it.

## The Patience of the Material

My furniture-building friend has a phrase for the feeling he gets when a piece comes together right: *the wood agreed*. It sounds mystical but it isn't—it's an acknowledgment that the material has constraints, preferences, a grain and a moisture content and a history that predates him and will outlast him. His job is to work with those properties, not against them.

Good writing has this too. There are sentences that want to be long and winding because the idea they carry is long and winding. There are arguments that need seven paragraphs of setup before they can land because that's how much context the reader needs to understand what's being claimed. You can strip that out in the name of concision and end up with something technically shorter and functionally useless.

Slowness isn't the goal. The goal is work that required the right amount of time—no more, no less. But in practice, for most of us, the error is almost always on the side of too fast. We mistake busyness for progress, output for quality, the sensation of motion for actual movement toward something.

## What Slowness Asks of You

Working slowly is not passive. It requires that you be willing to sit with uncertainty for longer than is comfortable. You have to resist the urge to close the question before it's actually closed—to arrive at a provisional answer and then keep pushing past it.

It also requires that you give up the pleasure of feeling productive in the conventional sense. Slow work often looks like nothing from the outside. You sit, you read the same paragraph a dozen times, you go for a walk, you sit again. There's no visible output. The work is happening anyway.

I've found that the best thing I can do when I'm stuck is to stop trying to solve the problem and instead try to understand it better. What is this actually about? What am I really trying to say? What would someone need to know to find this interesting? These questions seem to lead away from the work, but they usually lead directly through it.

The hour before the tool is not wasted time. It's where the work begins.
