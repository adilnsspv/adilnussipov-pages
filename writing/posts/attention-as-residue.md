---
title: Attention as Residue
description: What we pay attention to accumulates. The question is whether we're building something or just spending.
date: 2024-10-05
---

Someone told me once that you become what you repeatedly do. I've been thinking lately about a narrower version of that: you become what you repeatedly notice. Attention is not neutral. It changes you.

This is obvious in the large cases. People who spend years closely observing a single phenomenon—birds, markets, patients, code—develop a perceptual apparatus calibrated to that domain. They notice things that are invisible to the untrained eye. The attention has built something structural.

But I think it's also true in the small cases, the ambient cases, the kind of attention that's not directed at anything in particular. What you scroll through. What you overhear and absorb. What you glance at a hundred times a day without meaning to. This diffuse attention is building something too—or failing to build something. It's leaving residue.

## What Residue Looks Like

I can tell what I've been paying attention to by what I find myself thinking about in the shower. This is embarrassing to admit but probably universal: the mind, freed from any task, returns to whatever has been most persistently occupying it. For a while, this was news. Then, for a longer while, product decisions. Now, mostly, it's whatever I've been reading.

The shift happened slowly, without any deliberate choice on my part. I started protecting longer stretches of time for books. I started reading harder things, things that required me to be patient and re-read. And gradually the quality of my unstructured thinking changed. It became less reactive. The material the mind was working with was different.

I don't want to oversell this or make it sound like a self-improvement program, because it didn't feel like improvement at the time. It felt like loss. The news cycle stopped seeming urgent. Conversations about certain topics that used to feel important started feeling beside the point. This is possibly concerning—there are things you should care about—but it's also, I think, what it feels like when attention shifts to something with a longer time horizon.

## The Economics of Attention

Attention economists talk about attention as a scarce resource. This is true but misleading. The useful frame is not scarcity but *what you're building with it*.

Think of it as an investment rather than an expense. Every sustained act of attention toward something complex deposits something into a kind of cognitive account. You learn the domain. You develop intuitions. You start to see patterns. Distributed, shallow attention—the kind that platform design encourages—is the opposite. It keeps you liquid but poor. Lots of transactions, no accumulation.

The problem isn't that shallow attention is bad in itself. It's that it crowds out the deep kind. The two aren't easily concurrent. You have to choose, not once but continuously, and the choice is harder than it sounds because shallow attention is engineered to win.

## The Practice

I've become increasingly suspicious of anything that asks for my attention without giving something lasting in return. This is not a moral claim—I'm not sure there's anything wrong with amusement or distraction. But I've noticed that the texture of my days is better when more of my attention goes toward things that compound.

What compounds, for me: books, certain long-form writing, conversations with people who are working on hard things, time spent making things with my hands. What doesn't: most of what lives on the internet, most of what presents itself as news, most of what's designed to interrupt.

I'm not rigid about this. But I'm more deliberate than I used to be, and deliberateness is the thing. You have to decide what you want attention to leave behind. Because it will leave something. It always does.

The residue of a life is made of what you paid attention to.
