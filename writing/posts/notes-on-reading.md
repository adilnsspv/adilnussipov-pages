---
title: Notes on Reading Deeply
description: In defense of re-reading, marginalia, and books that take months to finish.
date: 2024-07-18
---

I have a book on my nightstand that I've been reading for four months. It's not particularly long—maybe four hundred pages—but it's dense, and I've been reading it slowly on purpose. A chapter at a time, sometimes a section at a time. I read other things during the day; this book is where I go in the evening, in the window between dinner and sleep, when my mind is tired enough to stop resisting and just follow.

I've read it before. This is the second time through.

Re-reading used to embarrass me. There are so many books I haven't read yet—why go back? But I've come to think of re-reading as one of the more honest acts available to a reader. It's an admission that you didn't get it all the first time. It's a return to something you thought you understood, to find out how much you missed.

## What You Miss the First Time

The first time through a difficult book you're mostly just surviving it—trying to keep track of the argument, the characters, the structure. You're reading for plot in the broadest sense: what happens next, where does this go, what is this about.

The second time you already know the answer to those questions. You're free to notice the texture. The way a particular sentence is constructed. The argument that seems to come from nowhere but was prepared thirty pages earlier. The image that keeps returning in different forms. First readings are mining expeditions; second readings are surveys of the terrain.

I've also found that I am a different reader than I was when I first read something. The books I first read in my twenties contained things I couldn't understand then—not because the words were hard, but because I hadn't lived enough to know what they were pointing at. Re-reading them now, I find them talking about things I recognize.

## The Technology of Marginalia

I write in my books. Not heavily—I'm not one of those readers whose margins are so dense they rival the text. But I mark passages, put question marks next to things I want to think about, write single words in the margins when something connects to something else I've been reading.

The practice forces a certain kind of engagement. You can't passively receive a book if you're also annotating it. You have to decide what matters, what connects, what you want to come back to. The pen keeps you honest.

When I come back to a book I've marked up, I'm reading two things: the original text and my past response to it. The marginalia is a kind of diary—a record of what I thought was important, what I was confused by, what I was excited about. Sometimes I agree with past-me. Often I don't. The disagreement is the most interesting part.

## On Difficulty

There's a category of books that are genuinely hard—not because they're badly written, but because the ideas they contain are genuinely difficult and can't be made easy without being made wrong. I've come to see engaging with this difficulty as one of the few genuinely productive forms of discomfort.

The difficulty is not a defect. It's a feature. It's the resistance that builds the capacity.

We have an ambient cultural preference for content that goes down easy. This preference is understandable—easy things are pleasant—but it has a cost. The books that have changed how I think are almost uniformly books that made me work. Books where I had to slow down, re-read, sit with uncertainty before the thing clicked. That clicking is not available any other way. You can't get to it by reading a summary.

## The Long Return

A book you read slowly and carefully becomes part of you in a way that a book you race through doesn't. This is not mystical; it's just a fact about how memory and integration work. Deep processing produces durable learning. Shallow processing produces the sensation of understanding, which is different from the thing.

Four months with a single book is not inefficient. It's a different measure of efficiency. I'm not trying to maximize the number of books I read. I'm trying to let a small number of good books do something to me.

There's a difference between having read a book and having been changed by it. The first is a fact about the past; the second is a fact about who you are now. I'm interested in the second kind.

Read slowly. Read twice. Read with a pen.
